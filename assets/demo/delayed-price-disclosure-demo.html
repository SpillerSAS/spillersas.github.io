The core insight: Delayed disclosure amplifies the direction set by store context. The delay in step (c) pushes the reference price further in whichever direction (b) already pointed. Premium context + delay = expectations drift high, making the real price feel like a bargain. Discount context + delay = expectations drift low, making the real price feel like a rip-off. Same product, same price — opposite reactions.

Implication for Pricing Strategy

Premium brands benefit from building desire before showing the price (the delay pushes expectations up, creating positive surprise). Value retailers should lead with the price — anchor the low price before the delay pushes expectations even lower.

Empirical Findings

Field Study: Mainstream Online Retailer

In a large-scale field experiment at a mainstream online retailer, delayed price disclosure decreased conversion rates:

2.61% → 2.31% (~11% relative decline)

Why? For a mainstream (non-premium) store, the delay pushed price expectations downward, making the actual price feel disappointing when finally revealed.

The Moderator: Store Positioning

The effect reverses in premium contexts. When consumers expect high prices, the delay pushes reference prices further upward, and the actual price creates positive disconfirmation.

Connection to Expectancy Disconfirmation

Recall the general satisfaction model: CS = CP − CE (satisfaction = perceived quality − expected quality). Delayed price disclosure works by manipulating CE — the consumer's expectation. The three-stage reference shift (a→b→c) is the mechanism by which the delay moves CE up or down, which then determines whether the price reveal produces positive or negative disconfirmation.

This effect is strongest for ambiguous product experiences — products where quality is hard to evaluate before purchase (headphones, wine, skincare). Ambiguity gives expectations more power over perceived quality, making the delay-driven expectation shift especially potent.

Managerial Takeaway

"Should we show the price upfront?" is the wrong question. The right question is: "In which direction will the delay push expectations — and will the reveal be a pleasant surprise or a letdown?"

Delay the price when: The store, brand, or product signals premium value. The delay lets expectations rise, making the actual price a pleasant surprise.
Show the price upfront when: You're positioned on value. Anchor the low price before the delay pushes expectations even lower.
